This is an interesting search. I should note upfront that the NISTIR 7330 models by Persily, Musser, and Leber (2006) were created primarily as CONTAM models for multizone airflow and IAQ analysis — not as EnergyPlus input files. So the direct use of those specific models in EnergyPlus by outside groups is limited. However, several non-NIST groups have done closely related work building their own representative U.S. housing stock models in EnergyPlus, often using the same underlying RECS and AHS data sources, and some cite NISTIR 7330 directly. Here's what I found:

---

**1. Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory (LBNL)**

Hopkins, Lekov, Lutz, Rosenquist, and Gu developed a tool that used EnergyPlus to simulate all ~3,418 single-family homes in the 2005 RECS dataset. This tool generates a calibrated, nationally representative set of simulated homes whose energy use is statistically indistinguishable from the actual RECS sample. They developed EnergyPlus models for a variety of HVAC technologies including electric, gas, and oil furnaces, central and window air conditioners, heat pumps, and baseboard heaters. This work explicitly builds on the same RECS foundation as NISTIR 7330.

**Citation:** Hopkins, A.S., Lekov, A., Lutz, J., Rosenquist, G., & Gu, L. (2011). *Simulating a Nationally Representative Housing Sample Using EnergyPlus*. LBNL-4220E. Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory.

---

**2. Carnegie Mellon University — Glasgo, Khan, and Azevedo**

This group used a batch simulation tool to translate energy audit records of 296 existing single-family homes in Pennsylvania into EnergyPlus building simulation models, comparing results to actual metered electricity data. They also reference and compare against NREL's ResStock tool which generated 350,000 EnergyPlus models of statistically representative homes across the U.S. Their reference list cites the LBNL/Hopkins work directly.

**Citation:** Glasgo, B., Khan, N., & Azevedo, I.L. (2020). Simulating a residential building stock to support regional efficiency policy. *Applied Energy*, 261, 114223.

---

**3. Carnegie Mellon University — Glasgo, Hendrickson, and Azevedo**

This earlier study assessed the ability of EnergyPlus to simulate 106 homes using audit records, homeowner surveys, and occupancy estimates, comparing simulated to actual building loads at the circuit level. They found that EnergyPlus models did not accurately predict individual home consumption, with most error attributed to appliance, lighting, and occupant behavior factors.

**Citation:** Glasgo, B., Hendrickson, C., & Azevedo, I.L. (2017). Assessing the value of information in residential building simulation: Comparing simulated and actual building loads at the circuit level. *Applied Energy*, 203, 348–363.

---

**4. University of Texas at Austin — Rhodes, Gorman, Upshaw, and Webber**

This group used BEopt (a GUI front-end for EnergyPlus developed by NREL) with energy audit data from 54 homes in Austin, TX that were part of a smart grid demonstration project. They found the software could estimate aggregate annual electrical energy use within about 1% for groups of homes, but individual home error could reach up to 28%.

**Citation:** Rhodes, J.D., Gorman, W.H., Upshaw, C.R., & Webber, M.E. (2015). Using BEopt (EnergyPlus) with energy audits and surveys to predict actual residential energy usage. *Energy and Buildings*, 86, 808–816.

---

**5. UT Austin / NREL — White, Rhodes, Wilson, and Webber**

This team used NREL's ResStock tool to develop geographically representative housing stock models and EnergyPlus to create an aggregate building stock energy model representing the Texas residential sector within the ERCOT grid region. They studied the impact of full electrification of space heating on electricity demand and peak power.

**Citation:** White, P.R., Rhodes, J.D., Wilson, E.J.H., & Webber, M.E. (2021). Quantifying the impact of residential space heating electrification on the Texas electric grid. *Applied Energy*, 298, 117113.

---

**6. NREL — ResStock (Wilson, Christensen, Horowitz, et al.)**

While NREL is a national lab (not strictly "outside NIST"), ResStock is built on OpenStudio/EnergyPlus and models existing residential building stocks at national, regional, or local scales with high granularity — roughly one physics-based simulation model for every 200 dwelling units. The ResStock team has run over 20 million simulations using a statistical model of housing stock characteristics, drawing from RECS and other data sources, similar in concept to the NISTIR 7330 approach but at far larger scale.

**Citation:** Wilson, E., Christensen, C., Horowitz, S., Robertson, J., & Maguire, J. (2017). *Electric End-Use Energy Efficiency Potential in the U.S. Single-Family Housing Stock*. NREL/TP-5500-65667.

---

**Key takeaway:** The NISTIR 7330 models were designed as CONTAM representations (for airflow/IAQ), so direct EnergyPlus adoption of those specific models by outside groups is rare. Instead, the broader approach — using RECS/AHS data to define statistically representative housing stock and then modeling in EnergyPlus — has been adopted independently by LBNL, Carnegie Mellon, UT Austin, and NREL, with these groups often citing NISTIR 7330 as foundational work for the concept. If you're specifically looking for groups who imported or adapted the NISTIR 7330 CONTAM models *into* EnergyPlus, that work has been done primarily within NIST itself through the CONTAM-EnergyPlus co-simulation effort.
